**How I choose what to recommend**: To recommend any course, I consider three main criteria:

1. I have done them personally and they have been useful for me to learn.
2. They are widely recommended by the community and are taught by referents in the industry.
3. They belong to universities or institutions of prestige, from which referents of high technical level have emerged.

Before entering the matter, a caveat that is worth having clear as soon as possible:

> Most online courses are *passive content*, that is, they do not force you to exercise what you have learned to pass to the next topic. I make this caveat because I consider that exercising is fundamental to learn. <br/> That said, I consider that they do serve as 'motivation' to keep discovering and learning.

## 2. University courses {2-university-courses}

While you are learning the basic syntax of some language, I suggest you complement it with a course on programming fundamentals. To start, the most recommended is:

1. [Harvard's CS50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gmuTjeQUbTM&pp=ygUMaGFydmFyZCBjczUw)

## 3. Passive technical content (*Infotainment*) {3-passive-technical-content-infotainment}

Here is where the range of resources grows exponentially. As I said before, knowing that these resources do not replace a *formal education* is fundamental. Nevertheless, they are very useful to keep motivation and discover new things. The platform par excellence is *YouTube*. I grouped the creators by type of contribution so you can choose according to what you need.

### General overview {general-overview}

Ideal if you are starting or you want a wide map of topics (*knowing what you don't know*).

1. [Tech with Tim](https://www.youtube.com/@TechWithTim): general programming, web development, fullstack, etc. His tutorials on languages helped me see what topics are covered *learning a programming language*.
2. [Corey Schafer](https://www.youtube.com/@CoreySchafer): a classic of YouTube. Excellent Python tutorials and very recommended; if you start from zero, it is an essential channel.
3. [freeCodeCamp](https://www.youtube.com/@freeCodeCamp): very useful tutorials when you start. With the rise of AI, the *tutorial of hours* format is less central, but it is still worth it. **That said:** beware of the *tutorial hell*.
4. [Fazt](https://www.youtube.com/@FaztTech) / [FaztCode](https://www.youtube.com/@FaztCode): first creator of Spanish-speaking content in the list. Fullstack at a glance and in detail. Entertaining videos, I like that he explains using diagrams. Whenever I see one of his videos, I come out with a list of topics to review or deepen.

### Python and ecosystem {python-and-ecosystem}

1. [NeuralNine](https://www.youtube.com/@NeuralNine): Python and libraries; often I find a tutorial about some tool that I had postponed and motivates me to keep exploring.
2. [Arjan Codes](https://www.youtube.com/@ArjanCodes): Python and best practices of development, mainly focused on *backend*; it has motivated me to explore more and rethink how I program.

### Algorithms and data structures {algorithms-and-data-structures}

1. [Neetcode](https://www.youtube.com/@Neetcode): algorithms and data structures, together with its platform [Neetcode.io](https://neetcode.io/) is an essential resource.

### Machine learning (technical content) {machine-learning-technical-content}

1. [Sentdex](https://www.youtube.com/@Sentdex): very technical, programming and applied mathematics to ML; a reminder of the level that can be reached with discipline.
2. [CodeEmporium](https://www.youtube.com/@CodeEmporium): concepts of ML in various areas, from mathematics to neural network architectures.

### Mathematics and statistics for ML {mathematics-and-statistics-for-ml}

1. [3Blue1Brown](https://www.youtube.com/@3Blue1Brown): mathematics explained with visualizations, I loved his videos when I deepened mathematics for ML. From that moment on I set myself to study mathematics more seriously.
2. [StatQuest](https://www.youtube.com/@Statquest): another classic of YouTube. Series on statistics, ML algorithms and deep learning. When a mathematical concept of ML doesn't fit in my head, I usually look for a video that helps me to unblock it.
3. [Steve Brunton](https://www.youtube.com/@SteveBrunton): advanced mathematics (calculus, linear algebra, statistics, probability, dynamic systems, etc.), also linked to ML. I love the videos where he writes on the board while explaining complex concepts. Completely recommended.
4. [Artem Kirsanov](https://www.youtube.com/@ArtemKirsanov): excellent animations, ML applied to other areas (e.g. neuroscience). Mathematics is demanding, so if you come with previous bases it becomes much more digestible.

### IA: referents, industry and trends {ia-referents-industry-and-trends}

1. [Andrej Karpathy](https://www.youtube.com/@AndrejKarpathy): worldwide referent in IA; content of very high level and very particular view. When he talks about the topic, it is worth listening.
2. [Tina Huang](https://www.youtube.com/@TinaHuang): very clear communication; good way to see what is happening in the United States and in the latest trends of IA. Invites to deepen in specific topics.
3. [Marina Wyss](https://www.youtube.com/@MarinaWyssAI): ML/IA; I like his *overviews* of trends and project ideas. I have taken several ideas from his videos.

### DevOps {devops}

1. [Tech World with Nana](https://www.youtube.com/@TechWorldwithNana): my favorite channel for DevOps. My knowledge in the area is still superficial, so each video is a new world. His way of exposing and the visualizations he uses make it very digestible.

### Backend (YouTube channel) {backend-youtube-channel}

1. [Boot.dev](https://www.youtube.com/@bootdotdev): in addition to the Python course I mentioned in [section 1](#1-online-courses-from-platforms), his channel has long courses on backend and short introductory videos.

### Software architecture and senior level (Spanish) {software-architecture-and-senior-level-spanish}

1. [Del código a la arquitectura](https://www.youtube.com/@CodigoArquitectura): Spanish-speaking channel; one of my lastest discoveries. Systems, programming paradigms and software architectures, topics that I didn't even know existed. Very useful if you are looking for *senior* concepts while you are traversing full stack development.

There are also channels like [IBM Technology](https://www.youtube.com/@IBMTechnology) that upload short but very well worked videos on specific technologies and [DeepLearning.ai](https://www.youtube.com/@Deeplearningai) that is a platform to learn Machine Learning and AI created by Andrew Ng.

I could go on indefinitely but I think that with these resources are enough. Along your way you will find your own content creators that resonate with your interests and I recommend following them. For those moments of low or doubts, rediscovering what you were interested in at the beginning will help you stay motivated and continue this long learning path.
